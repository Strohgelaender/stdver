Standard Versioning Scheme
Scheme
The Standard Versioning is a maturity-oriented versioning scheme, defined by the following version identifier scheme:
M.NpR [.D] [+H] [-S]
Parts
The parts of the Standard Versioning version identifier scheme are:
  • M: /\d+/ (M ≥ 0) — Major Version
    Major version of solution. Bumped on major changes, like full modernizations. A bump resets the Minor Version N and Revision R to 0. On change, backward compatibility is not possible. On upgrade, human interaction is expected. Rationale: Primary version of solution.
  • N: /\d+/ (N ≥ 0) — Minor Version
    Minor version of the solution within the Major Version. Bumped on minor changes, like new features. A bump resets the Revision R to 0. On change, backward compatibility is seeked for, but not assured. On upgrade, human interaction might be required. Rationale: Secondary version of solution.
  • p: /(a|b|rc|\.)/ — Maturity Phase
    Maturity phase within the combination of Major Version M and Minor Version N. A change resets the Revision R to 0. The distinct phases, in chronological order, are:
    • a: Alpha
      Early version of the solution with incomplete and unstable functionalities, to get feedback on solution.
    • b: Beta
      Early version of the solution with complete but still unstable functionalities, to stabilize solution.
    • rc: Release Candidate
      Mature version of the solution with complete and stable functionalities, to catch final inconsistencies just before release.
    • .: Release
      Mature version of the solution with complete and stable functionalities, available for full production use.
    Rationale: Integrate maturity phase.
  • R: /\d+/ (R ≥ 0) — Phase Revision
    Revision of the Maturity Phase within the combination of Major Version M and Minor Version N. Bumped for every revision within a Maturity Phase p. On change within Alpha and Beta maturity phases, no backward compatibility has to be assured. On change within Release Candidate and Release maturity phases, full backward compatibility has to be assured. On upgrade, no human interaction is required. Rationale: Revision counter within maturity phase.
  • D: /\d{8}/ — Release Date
    The optional release date of year, month and date, represented in sort-supporting ISO notation. Rationale: Support snapshots between official versions.
  • H: /[\dA-F]{4}/ — Source Hash
    The optional 16-bit hashing checksum of the source code of the solution, represented as a upper-case hexadecimal value. This usually is an, automatically calculated build-time, SHA1 or MD5 hash value (8 bytes, 128 bit), folded 3 times through an XOR-operation into just 2 bytes / 16 bit. Rationale: Support sub-daily snapshots and easy build identification during developer hot-reload round-trips.
  • S: /(XA|LA|EA|GA)/ — Availability Scope
    The optional availability scopes of the release. The known scopes are:
    • XA: No Availability
      No public availability of solution at all. The scope for all Development and sometimes Snapshot point-in-times.
    • LA: Limited Availability
      Limited public availability of solution. Usually for releases after the End-of-Life-Announcement (EOLA) or for releases with specific customer features.
    • EA: Early Availability
      Early public availability of solution for early market. Usually for Beta or Release Candidate levels or for Release and initial Release Update levels.
    • GA: General Availability
      Late public availability of solution for mainstream market. Usually for Release and sometimes just for Release Update levels.
    Rationale: Support intended scope of version.
Level
The Standard Versioning version identifier scheme can be used at the following three distinct levels in practice:
  • Level 0: M.N.R
    This uses Standard Versioning, just with release revisions and excluding the optional information.
  • Level 1: M.NpR
    This uses Standard Versioning, excluding the optional information.
  • Level 2: M.NpR [.D] [+H] [-S]
    This uses Standard Versioning, including the optional information.
Standard Versioning Decoding
Online Decoder

To decode a Standard Versioning identifier, you can just prefix it with https://stdver.org#.

Example: https://stdver.org#1.2.3.20230810+ABCD-XA

Regular Expressions

To match a Standard Versioning identifier, one can use the following Regular Expression (RegExp):

\d+\.\d+
(?:a|b|rc|\.)\d+
(?:\.\d{8})?
(?:\+[\dA-F]{4})?
(?:-(?:XA|LA|EA|GA))?

To match and decode a Standard Versioning identifier, one can use the following Regular Expression (RegExp) which contains the necessary capture groups:

(\d+)\.(\d+)
(a|b|rc|\.)(\d+)
(?:\.(\d{8}))?
(?:\+([\dA-F]{4}))?
(?:-(XA|LA|EA|GA))?

Alternative Versioning Schemes
Beside Standard Versioning there are other popular versioning schemes, each with their pros and cons and with distinct differences to Standard Versioning.
  • Semantic Versioning

    Semantic Versioning is strict versioning scheme, based on three version numbers where each version number is incremented when a certain class of change was done. The types of changes are backward compatible bugfixes, backward compatible new functionality, and backward incompatible changes. Hence, Semantic Versioning primary is compatibility-driven.

    PRO: One knows exactly what compatibility to expect from such a version number. Its base part of the versions also follows the widespread scheme of three numbers and this way meets the representation expectation of many people.

    CON: The compatibility-driven semantics are useful in practice, but when it comes to version numbers, the intuition of many people is primarily maurity-oriented and just secondarily compatibility-driven. Also, the maturity phase (pre-release) information is just attached instead of fully integrated and hence confusing for some edge cases. For instance, 1.2.3-alpha.4 would denoate the 4th alpha of version 1.2.3, but as the 3 of 1.2.3 in Semantic Versioning is a strict patchlevel for fully compatible bugfixes, why should an alpha version exists for such a situation anyway?

  • Calendrical Versioning

    Calendrical version identifiers like 2023.08 are a versioning scheme, usually based on year and month information and in ISO-notation.

    PRO: Calendrical version identifiers combine the intuition of traditional double-digit based versions with the release point in time.

    CON: Because of the maximum of a month resolution, they fit primarily large products with long release-cycles only, and especially do not work for small release-cycles.

  • Marketing Versioning

    Marketing version identifiers like (Windows) Vista, (macOS) Ventura or (Debian GNU/Linux) Bookworm are based on code-names, usually derived from non-reserved/non-registerable names of places, people, etc.

    PRO: Those version identifiers are nice in marketing products, as they can be easily pronounced and are catchy.

    CON: Those version identifiers carry no maturity and/or compatibility semantics at all and hence in practice always have to be combined with underlying technical version identifiers.

About Standard Versioning
The Standard Versioning dates back to early definitions in the year 2008 by Dr. Ralf S. Engelschall in the context of its Software Architecture trainings. It was formally defined and publically published on stdver.org in 2023 to have a convenient reference at hand on the Web.

Standard Versioning is motivated by the usual maturity-based intution most people have in mind when it comes to solution versions. It also integrates the recurring revisions of maturity phases directly into the version. Finally, it knows the optional tagging of versions with release date, source hash and availability scope in order to support snapshot versions, development versions and different target customer scopes.
